# The Hemisphere Is Ours: The Monroe Doctrine Then and Now

In December 1823, President James Monroe stood before Congress and delivered what would become one of the most consequential foreign policy declarations in American history. In a message drafted largely by Secretary of State John Quincy Adams, Monroe asserted that the Western Hemisphere was closed to future European colonization, and that any attempt by Old World powers to extend their political systems into the Americas would be considered a threat to the peace and safety of the United States. In return, the young republic pledged to remain aloof from European wars and internal affairs. The principle was modest in its original ambition and audacious in its scope — a proclamation made by a nation with, as historian Lindsay Chervinsky has noted, "a tiny baby navy and a tiny baby army" that had little means of enforcing it at the time.

Two centuries later, the spirit of that declaration has not only survived but has been dramatically expanded, repackaged, and actively enforced in ways Monroe and Adams could scarcely have imagined. The final draft of the 2025 National Security Strategy called upon the United States to "reassert and enforce the Monroe Doctrine to restore American pre-eminence in the Western Hemisphere," and the Trump administration has since demonstrated that this language represents genuine policy intent rather than rhetorical flourish. Trump's efforts to rename the Gulf of Mexico, pursue acquisition of Canada, the Panama Canal, and Greenland, as well as conducting military action against Venezuela, have all been cited as components of what the administration calls the "Trump Corollary" to the Monroe Doctrine — or, in Trump's own preferred branding, the "Donroe Doctrine." Comparing the original 1823 doctrine to its modern iteration reveals both a continuous thread of American strategic thinking and a dramatic — and troubling — evolution in the means and ends it now serves.

## Origins and Intent

To understand how far the doctrine has traveled, it helps to understand what it was originally designed to do. The geopolitical context of 1823 was specific and urgent. Most of Spain's colonial possessions in Latin America had recently achieved independence, and there were fears — partly stoked by the Holy Alliance of European monarchies — that Spain might attempt to reclaim them with outside assistance. Monroe wanted both to prevent Europe from reclaiming control and to assert U.S. influence in the hemisphere. The declaration served, in essence, as an extended diplomatic warning shot: the Americas were no longer available real estate for imperial competition.

Crucially, the doctrine as Adams conceived it was largely defensive in character. Adams believed that republics shared values, and shared values made the best allies. If the United States had possessed the military might to enforce the Monroe Doctrine, it was mostly a defensive document — he wanted to protect the emerging republics in South and Central America as potential allies, but not intervene to enforce republicanism in other nations. This is worth holding in mind. The architect of the doctrine did not envision it as license for the United States to police, manipulate, or overthrow governments throughout the hemisphere. He was concerned with hard power — armies on the ground, navies in coastal waters — not with economic dominance or political coercion of neighbors. The doctrine was also notably limited in its economic dimension. Adams and his contemporaries were much less concerned about trade and economic influence; they didn't view trade as a zero-sum game and did not see European trade in South America as reducing American influence and therefore a threat to American security.

That relatively restrained vision did not survive the century intact.

## The Corollaries That Changed Everything

The original Monroe Doctrine was transformed into something far more interventionist through a series of presidential additions that compounded its scope. The most consequential was Theodore Roosevelt's 1904 corollary, which asserted that the United States had the right to intervene militarily in Latin American nations that failed to maintain order or honor their international obligations. In other words, the Western Hemisphere was still closed to colonization by European powers, but the U.S. might be required to intervene to protect lives and property, and preserve order in its proverbial backyard. This reframing transformed the Monroe Doctrine from a shield against European imperialism into a justification for American imperialism. The United States proceeded to occupy Nicaragua, Haiti, the Dominican Republic, and Cuba under this logic, operating as a self-appointed regional constable.

The Roosevelt Corollary was eventually moderated. Franklin Roosevelt's "Good Neighbor Policy" renounced the right of unilateral intervention and helped co-found the Organization of American States. For several decades in the mid-twentieth century, the formal doctrine was softened into a framework of multilateral consultation — though in practice, Cold War imperatives led the United States to support coups, fund insurgencies, and topple democratically elected governments whenever leftist governments threatened to align with the Soviet Union. The doctrine's letter changed; its underlying logic of hemispheric dominance did not.

## The Trump Corollary: Monroe Meets the 21st Century

What is novel about the current moment is not the underlying ambition — that is thoroughly continuous with American history — but the explicitness with which it is now asserted and the range of instruments being deployed to achieve it. According to the Trump Corollary, the U.S. has the right to resuscitate the Monroe Doctrine: adjusting its military presence in the region, increasing naval forces to control migrant routes and illicit trafficking, and carrying out border deployments. The strategy also commits to using "the military system superior to any other country in the world" to gain access to energy and mineral resources in the region. This final point represents perhaps the most significant departure from the original doctrine. Adams emphatically did not see European economic activity in Latin America as a threat. Trump's NSS explicitly frames resource access — critical minerals, oil, supply chains — as a core national security interest to be secured by any means necessary.

The Venezuela operation crystallized how seriously the administration takes this posture. Following the capture of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro in a January 2026 raid, Trump claimed that the action was an application of the Monroe Doctrine, stylizing it as the "Donroe Doctrine" and telling reporters that "American dominance in the Western Hemisphere will never be questioned again." The operation, codenamed Operation Absolute Resolve, was framed publicly as a law enforcement action targeting a "narco-terrorist" — echoing the legal rationale used in 1989 when George H.W. Bush ordered the invasion of Panama to capture Manuel Noriega. This thin veil of legality has proved successful in the past, and the Trump administration has employed it with confidence. Defense Secretary Hegseth was notably candid about the broader goals, describing the administration's aim of "reestablishing American deterrence and dominance in the Western Hemisphere" and warning that "our adversaries remain on notice — America can project our will anywhere, anytime."

What the Venezuela action also illuminated was the naked economic dimension beneath the security rhetoric. For the U.S. president, "cooperation" with Venezuela's new government would involve giving U.S. oil companies unfettered access to Venezuela's oil reserves, the largest in the world. Monroe's doctrine said nothing about American commercial prerogatives in the hemisphere; the Donroe Doctrine makes them central.

## The China Factor: A New Old World Power

The original Monroe Doctrine was directed at European monarchies seeking to extend their reach into the Americas. The current revival of the doctrine is directed, in significant part, at the People's Republic of China — which, from Washington's perspective, represents precisely the kind of extra-hemispheric power that the doctrine was designed to exclude. China's December 2025 Policy Paper explicitly designated Latin America as central to Belt and Road expansion and the Global Development Initiative, prioritizing infrastructure development, manufacturing cooperation, and technology transfer, positioning the region as a key outlet for China's industrial transition to higher-value sectors.

The essence of the newly invigorated Trump Corollary is that there is no room for multipolarity in the Western Hemisphere. Trump has declared that the U.S. "will reassert and enforce the Monroe Doctrine to restore American preeminence in the Western Hemisphere, and to protect our homeland and our access to key geographies throughout the region — deny non-Hemispheric competitors the ability to position forces or other threatening capabilities, or to own or control strategically vital assets, in our Hemisphere."

Chinese policy experts have sounded alarm about what they term "New Monroism," and Beijing's response to the Venezuela raid was immediate and unambiguous — characterizing the operation as a hegemonic act that fundamentally violates international law and UN Charter principles. There is a particular irony in Beijing's complaint, given that China has pursued its own sphere-of-influence logic in the South China Sea and Taiwan Strait. But the parallels between the two powers' approaches to regional dominance have not been lost on analysts, with Western and Taiwanese observers warning that U.S. blockade tactics against Venezuela could provide rhetorical justification for similar coercive measures against Taiwan.

## Continuities, Departures, and the Question of Legitimacy

Comparing the 1823 doctrine to its current incarnation reveals three layers of continuity and two significant departures. The continuities are clear: the hemisphere-as-sphere-of-influence logic, the reflexive suspicion of extra-regional powers, and the willingness to use the language of protection and security to justify interventionism. What Adams began as a defensive posture, subsequent administrations have repeatedly weaponized as an offensive tool whenever American interests — strategic, ideological, or commercial — demanded it. In this sense, the Donroe Doctrine is not an aberration. It is the latest iteration of a pattern that runs from the Roosevelt Corollary through the Cold War and into the present.

The departures are nevertheless significant. First, the original doctrine operated within a framework — however imperfect — of respect for sovereignty among the independent republics it claimed to protect. Adams explicitly did not want to impose American governance models on neighboring nations by force. The current doctrine has no such restraint. Historian Gretchen Murphy of the University of Texas described Trump's invocation of the doctrine as consistent with how predecessors used it to "police Latin American nations to make sure their governments acted in U.S. commercial and strategic interests," adding that "Trump is jumping on this familiar pattern — citing the Monroe Doctrine to legitimate interventions that undermine real democracy, and ones where various kinds of interests are served, including commercial interests."

Second, the scale of ambition is genuinely new. The Monroe Doctrine was originally about preventing European colonization. The Donroe Doctrine describes attempts to rename international bodies of water, pursue acquisition of a NATO ally's autonomous territory in Greenland, pressure Canada toward political integration, seize the Panama Canal Zone, and capture a sitting foreign head of state — all within the space of a single presidential term. The doctrine has gone from a defensive perimeter to an expansionist blueprint.

## Regional Reactions and the International Order

The hemisphere's reaction to all of this has been mixed, reflecting the diversity of political conditions across Latin America. Trump has repeatedly praised Argentine President Javier Milei, who was the first foreign leader to meet with Trump after winning the 2024 election and came out in support of the U.S. capture of Maduro in January 2026. The ideological alignment between the two administrations has made Argentina something of a junior partner in the new hemispheric order. Colombia, by contrast, has faced open hostility — Trump accused Colombian President Gustavo Petro of being an "illegal drug dealer" and cut off diplomatic aid to the country.

The broader implications for international order are profound. Commentators have referred to this as an era of "rogue superpowers" and the "Putinisation" of U.S. foreign policy, a comparison that cuts in complicated directions. The argument is not that the United States has become Russia — the democratic institutions, however strained, remain qualitatively different — but that the United States under its current doctrine has effectively reverted to a pre-rules-based order logic in which great powers maintain spheres of influence secured by force, and international law becomes a convenience rather than a constraint. For Beijing, the Venezuela operation delivers a clear message: the U.S. no longer views international law as a binding constraint on its power.

## Conclusion

James Monroe and John Quincy Adams would likely recognize the strategic logic at the heart of what is being done in their name, even if they would be startled by its scale and methods. The fundamental premise — that the Western Hemisphere constitutes a zone of American primacy that must be defended against rival great powers — is perfectly continuous with what they articulated in 1823. The means available to enforce it are, of course, incomparably greater.

What they would not recognize — and what the historical record suggests they did not intend — is an American foreign policy that abandons the pretense of hemispheric partnership in favor of naked dominance, that treats resource access as a security imperative justifying military force, and that openly frames the seizure of foreign leaders and the acquisition of allied territories as legitimate instruments of statecraft. The Monroe Doctrine was, at its best, an assertion of American security interest that could coexist with genuine respect for the sovereignty of neighboring republics. The Donroe Doctrine appears to view sovereignty as an obstacle rather than a value.

Whether this aggressive reassertion of hemispheric hegemony ultimately produces the security and prosperity its architects promise, or whether it destabilizes a region already battered by inequality and democratic erosion, will be one of the defining geopolitical questions of the coming decade. What is already clear is that the hemisphere is being reshaped — and that the ghost of 1823 has been summoned to bless changes that its original authors never envisioned and might not have sanctioned.
